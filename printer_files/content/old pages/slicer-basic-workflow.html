Basic Workflow

The following pages explore some of PrusaSlicer’s many features, which allow you to customize your prints in a variety of ways. Understanding these tools gives you a high level of control over the printing process and can significantly improve the quality of your results.

That said, the software can feel overwhelming at first, especially for beginners. To make the process more manageable, we will start by guiding you step by step through PrusaSlicer’s basic workflow before building toward a few of the more advanced features.

Import the Model

Before you can print anything, you need a 3D model. You can either download models from online libraries like Printables or Thingiverse, or create your own using CAD software such as Autodesk Fusion.

First, you need to save your model to a location on your computer that you can easily find.

There are several ways to import the model into PrusaSlicer:

  • Click Add to open your file explorer and import your file (recommended).
  • Go to File → Import → Import STL/3MF/STEP/OBJ/AMF...
  • Drag and drop a file directly into the PrusaSlicer window.

Regardless of whichever method you choose, the model will end up on your print plate.

Inspect the Model

Before proceeding it is important to inspect your model from all angles to make sure that it imported correctly.

Left-click and drag to rotate the view.

Right-click and drag to pan the view.

Scroll with the mouse wheel to zoom in and out.

Position the Model

To ensure a successful print, your model should typically have its largest flat surface touching the print bed.

If the model imported at an angle that is not optimal for printing you can use the rotate tool to manually adjust its orientation, or you can select the Place on Face tool, then click one of the highlighted faces to quickly snap the model into the correct position.

You can optionally move the model around with your mouse, but leaving it in the middle of the printing surface is usually best. The exact position on the plate does not matter as long as no part of the model is out of the printing area. In that case, the program will warn you, and the model will turn blue.

Select the Printer, Filament, and Preset Settings

Now, you have to specify three settings from the right panel (preferably in this order):

  1. Printer
  2. Filament
  3. Print settings

You will primarily have access to Prusa MK4S printers with HF0.4mm nozzles. We do have additional printers and accessories in the makerspace, but those require additional training to be able to use them.

The Print settings primarily affect the layer height, which is typically between 0.10 and 0.28 mm. Smaller layer heights offer more detail but take longer to print. A good choice is typically one of the 0.20mm profiles, which provide a good balance of speed and quality for most use cases.

Enable Supports

A 3D printer cannot reliably print into thin air. Choose whether to generate supports, i.e. printed “scaffolding” which increases reliability when printing overhangs.

Select Supports → Everywhere if you are unsure. This may increase print time, but helps prevent failures.

Brim and Infill

You also have the option to change the object's infill density. The default value of 15% is sufficient for most prints.

If only a small part of your object is touching the print bed, you might want to enable brim — a thin outline around the base that increases contact area and helps adhesion.

Slice and Preview

Click Slice now to generate a preview of the print.

VERY IMPORTANT: Use the layer slider to inspect the print. This often reveals problems before printing.

Export and Print

After checking the preview, export the G-code. The preferred method is to insert a USB drive and export directly to it.

Finally, prepare your printer (clean the bed and load filament) and start the print.

Interface: Right Side Panel

Next we will look at the panel on the right side of the screen, which is the most important part of the program. As we saw in the basic workflow page, it is used for every print job. All the fundamental settings are placed in this part of the screen: selecting the layer height, print material, printer type, enabling supports, and choosing the infill density. Below all the settings, there is the Object List, which includes all the models in the project.

When setting up a print, it is important to change the settings in the following order.

Printer

Choose the printer for this job. You should always select the printer first because it overrides all the other settings. For this training, we will focus on the standard MK4S. There are other printers and accessories available, but those will require additional training before you are able to use them.

Filament

Different materials require different settings, such as temperature and print speed, so it is important to choose the correct one. We have many different colors of Overture PLA available for you to use in the makerspace. Additionally, we have a few spools of PETG and TPU available.

If you need to use a material other than the ones provided, you need to confirm that it is okay before using it. Some materials, such as ABS, are not allowed in the space.

Print Settings

This section allows you to quickly select one of the presets for the selected printer and filament. From the dropdown menu, you can choose from various print layer heights. Even though these presets are named after the layer height, each one also includes many other settings that influence quality and print time.

The lower the layer height, the better the details, but the print process will take significantly longer. The 0.2 mm layer height usually offers a good tradeoff between quality and speed for most projects.

Supports

A 3D printer cannot reliably print into thin air. As the material builds up layer by layer, each layer has to have at least a bit of a solid base underneath.

The parts of the model that are hanging or protruding “in the air” are called overhangs. Whether these overhangs are printable depends on their size and angle. Roughly speaking, 45° is okay, like the arms of the letter “Y,” but 90° is not, like the arms of the letter “T.”

Generating supports, or printed scaffolding, increases reliability when printing overhangs. It can be difficult for beginners to assess whether supports are needed. When downloading a model from sites like Printables, the model authors usually leave instructions about whether supports are needed, so make sure to read the model descriptions.

Select Supports → Everywhere if you are unsure about supports and there is no helpful comment on the model page. This might result in longer print time and extra post-processing, but it will help prevent the print from failing partway through.

Many times, you can eliminate the need for supports by altering the orientation of the model to reduce or remove overhangs.

Infill

3D models are rarely printed fully solid on the inside. A 100% density print would take a very long time, consume a lot of material, and make the object heavy. Instead, most prints are hollow with a supporting mesh inside called infill.

Objects with higher infill density are somewhat tougher. Some mechanical parts are printed with 90–100% infill, but most of the time it does not make sense to go above about 40%. A setting of 15–20% is usually a good tradeoff between toughness, reliability, print time, and material use. Many prints can even work with as little as 5%.

If you want to improve toughness, increasing the number of perimeters is usually more effective than adding infill density. We will explore this in the Object List panel below.

Infill can also use various patterns. The two patterns you will use most frequently are Grid and Gyroid.

Grid is the standard infill pattern used in most preset settings. It is reliable, quick to print, and uses relatively little filament. Grid works well as a default option, especially for nonfunctional parts where strength is not the main concern.

Gyroid is an advanced infill pattern that creates a smooth, continuous 3D structure. It provides strength in all directions and offers a strong balance of durability, flexibility, and material use. It may take slightly longer to print than grid, but it is a great choice for functional parts.

Brim

The Brim option adds a thin layer of material that extends outward from the base of your print, creating a larger surface area that sticks to the build plate. This improves bed adhesion and reduces the chances of warping or the print coming loose, especially for parts with small footprints or sharp corners.

A brim is typically easy to remove after printing and uses only a small amount of extra filament.

A brim is typically needed when printing tall or narrow parts, or when working with materials that are more prone to warping. If you are unsure, it is usually better to enable a brim than to risk the print failing due to poor bed adhesion.

Object List Panel

In the Object List panel, you will see all of the models currently placed on the print bed. For simple prints, this list may only contain a single item. For more complex projects, the list can include multiple parts and nested components.

Selecting a model, either directly on the print plate or by clicking its name in the Object List, will open a panel below the list with additional controls. This panel allows you to precisely adjust the position, rotation, and scale of your model by entering exact values instead of relying on dragging manually.

If you make any adjustments to the scale or rotation, you can reverse them by clicking the small circular arrow icon next to the modified values. Also, make sure to keep the ratios locked with the padlock icon so the model scales uniformly instead of deforming.

Right-clicking on an object, either on the print plate or in the Object List panel, opens a menu with additional settings that allow you to customize and edit your model. We will not go through most of these options in this training, but you are encouraged to explore them on your own.

The two options we will focus on are Infill and Layers and Perimeters. Selecting either of these will open a new panel below the Object List.

The Infill panel gives you greater control over both infill density and pattern. For most nonfunctional prints, 15%–20% Grid infill is a good default. For stronger functional parts, you can increase infill to around 40% using the Gyroid pattern. In general, avoid going above 50% infill because it adds print time and filament without much extra strength.

The Layers and Perimeters panel allows you to adjust the number of top and bottom layers, layer height, and the number of perimeters. Layer height has the greatest impact on print quality. A value of 0.20 mm is reliable for most prints.

For a successful print, use at least 5 top layers, 3 bottom layers, and 2 perimeters. Increasing these values to 6–8 top and bottom layers and 4–6 perimeters can significantly improve part strength and durability.

Slice Button

The final option in the right-side panel is the Slice button at the bottom. This applies all of the settings you selected and converts the model into G-code, which the printer uses to create the part. We will explore this process in more detail in a later section.

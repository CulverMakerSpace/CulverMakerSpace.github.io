Basic Workflow

The following pages explore some of PrusaSlicer’s many features, which allow you to customize your prints in a variety of ways. Understanding these tools gives you a high level of control over the printing process and can significantly improve the quality of your results.

That said, the software can feel overwhelming at first, especially for beginners. To make the process more manageable, we will start by guiding you step by step through PrusaSlicer’s basic workflow before building toward a few of the more advanced features.

Import the Model

Before you can print anything, you need a 3D model. You can either download models from online libraries like Printables or Thingiverse, or create your own using CAD software such as Autodesk Fusion.

First, save your model to a location on your computer that you can easily find.

  • Click Add, which is the only active button in the top menu bar.
  • Go to File → Import → Import STL/3MF/STEP/OBJ/AMF...
  • Drag and drop a file directly into the PrusaSlicer window.

Regardless of which method you choose, the model will end up on your print plate.

Inspect the Model

Left-click and drag anywhere outside a model to rotate the view.

Right-click and drag to pan the view.

Scroll with the mouse wheel to zoom in and out.

Position the Model

To ensure a successful print, your model should typically have its largest flat surface touching the print bed.

If the model imported at an angle that is not optimal for printing, use the rotate tool or Place on Face tool to snap it into the correct position.

You can optionally move the model around with your mouse, but leaving it in the middle of the printing surface is usually best. The exact position on the plate does not matter as long as no part of the model is out of the printing area. In that case, the program will warn you, and the model will turn blue.

Select the Printer, Filament, and Preset Settings

Now, you have to specify three settings from the right panel (preferably in this order):

  1. the"Printer"
  2. the"Filament"
  3. the initial"Print settings"

You will primarily have access to Prusa MK4S printers with HF0.4mm nozzles.

Always select the correct material! Each material has specific temperature, speed, and flow rate requirents. Printing with incorrect settings can lead to serious technical issues.

The Print settings primarily affect the layer height, which is typically between 0.10 and 0.28 mm. Smaller layer heights offer more detail but take longer to print. A good choice is typically one of the 0.20mm profiles, which provide a good balance of speed and quality for most use cases.

Enable Supports

A 3D printer cannot reliably print into thin air. Choose whether to generate supports, i.e. printed “scaffolding” which increases reliability when printing overhangs.

Select Supports → Everywhere if you are unsure if the model needs supports or not. This will result in a longer print time and some extra post-processing, but it will ensure that the print doesn’t fail partway through.

Brim and Infill

You also have the option to change the object's infill density (i.e., how much material should be on the inside). However, the default value of 15% is sufficient for most prints.

If only a small part of your object is touching the print bed, you also need to enable brim - a thin outline around the base of the object that increases the contact area and helps the part stick to the print surface.

As with supports, if you are unsure about the amount of print surface contact, enable the Brim setting.

Slice and Preview

Click Slice now button in the bottom right corner of the workspace to generate a preview of what the printer will produce as well as useful information about print time and the amount of material needed.

VERY IMPORTANT: Use the vertical slider on the right to inspect the individual layers of the print before sending it to the printer.

Export and Print

Export the G-code to a USB drive or your computer, then prepare the printer and start the print.
